# Voice & Style Guide: esahakulinen.fi

> Reference document for all content on the site. Read this before writing or editing any copy.

## Core Identity

Esa Hakulinen is a digital marketer. He uses AI coding tools to turn ideas into working products. The site is a portfolio for sharing projects and ideas. Expertise is demonstrated through the work itself, not through claims or self-promotion.

Background context (not for public copy): Esa has a minor in computer science, works in marketing operations and campaign management, and is interested in how design decisions shape user behavior. He prefers open-source tools, dislikes low-value SaaS subscriptions, and believes in doing things properly. These traits should emerge through project write-ups and blog posts, never through taglines or declarations.

## Positioning (Internal Only)

This site is a work in progress. It contains whatever Esa has had the time and interest to put together. It is not a complete archive, not a commitment to document everything, and not a statement about what matters. It's just what happens to be here.

This nonchalance is intentional. The site should never promise completeness, imply a system, or justify itself. Some projects get written up. Some don't. There is no philosophy behind which ones make it here; it's just what got done.

## Voice Principles

### 1. Show, Don't Promote (and Don't Overreach)

Never explicitly state "I'm good at this." Let the projects, the thinking, and the results do that work. Avoid superlatives about yourself. Avoid phrases like "I'm passionate about" or "I love experimenting." Instead, just experiment.

Equally important: don't claim expertise the reader has no reason to believe yet. "I work at the intersection of marketing, human behavior, and technology" sounds pretentious as an opening line. Those are conclusions the reader should arrive at after seeing the work. Start with the simple, honest label (digital marketer) and let the depth emerge through projects and writing.

The rule: give people snippets of information at a time, and through those snippets, build credibility. Underlining things for the sake of it is pretentious.

Bad: "Marketing, human behavior, and technology." (as a tagline)
Good: "Digital marketer. Welcome to my portfolio site, where I share projects and ideas." (then the projects demonstrate the depth)

### 2. Intentional and Precise

Esa is a designer. He thinks about why things work, not just what works. Copy should reflect deliberate choices and clear reasoning. Avoid vague language. Be specific about decisions and their rationale.

Bad: "I built a cool tool that helps with campaigns."
Good: "Campaign naming chaos costs hours in reporting. Dropdowns over free text solved it: when people select from a list, they conform."

### 3. Design Thinking Lives in Projects, Not in Declarations

A core belief: design is a conversation between the designer and the user. Technology enables it. The emotional experience matters as much as the functional outcome. Frictionless design and technology working together is the goal.

But this philosophy should never be stated as a standalone declaration. It belongs in project write-ups, where the reader can see it in action. Walk through the process: why this layout, why this content architecture, why this decision over that one. The reader should understand the design thinking by following the reasoning, not by reading a manifesto.

The about page, homepage, and other early-journey pages should stay pragmatic and factual. Design philosophy emerges in the deeper content where there is actual context for it.

### 4. Open by Default

Preference for openness, free and open-source solutions, and delivering value without paywalls or artificial scarcity. Skeptical of low-cost SaaS subscriptions that fragment tools and attention. This shapes tool choices and project philosophy; it should show in the work descriptions.

### 5. Do Things Properly

Organization, best practices, and doing things by the book matter. Not because of rigidity, but because good structure enables good work. This shows up in how projects are documented, how code is organized, and how problems are approached.

## Tone

Straightforward. Almost blunt. The trust comes from saying exactly what something is, without dressing it up.

There is a dry humor in stating the obvious. "Some of them are useful. Some of them are not." That deadpan quality delights people who share the same sensibility and signals honesty to everyone else. Don't try to be funny. Just be direct, and the humor emerges on its own.

Never poetic. Never wise-sounding. If a sentence could appear on a motivational poster, rewrite it.

## What to Avoid

- Self-promotional language ("I'm an expert in...", "my unique approach...")
- Hedging and false modesty ("I'm just a...", "hopefully this helps someone")
- Buzzwords without substance ("leveraging synergies", "growth hacking")
- Title case in headings, buttons, and labels. Use sentence case: capitalize only the first letter of the first word. "Latest projects" not "Latest Projects." "Key learnings" not "Key Learnings." Single-word labels (Home, Blog, About) are fine as-is.
- Em dashes and en dashes (use commas, colons, semicolons, parentheses, or periods instead)
- Exclamation marks (rarely, if ever)
- Generic marketing speak ("take your X to the next level")
- Over-explaining motivations ("the reason I did this is because I wanted to learn")
- Romanticizing the process ("the process is the point," "every project starts as an idea worth testing," "insights from my journey"). State things plainly. If a section is just a bridge to another page, make it a bridge. Don't turn it into a manifesto. Words like "journey," "insights," and "thoughts" dress up simple things. A blog is a blog. Posts are posts.
- Sneaking in value judgments ("because that's the honest thing to do," "because it matters"). These frame personal choices as moral positions. Just state what you do.
- Implicit promises or commitments ("I document both," "I always share my process"). The site is just what's here. It doesn't promise completeness or consistency. Avoid any phrasing that sounds like a personal rule or manifesto about what you will or won't do.

## Opinions: Where and When

The site pages (home, about, projects listing) should be neutral and factual. They describe what the site is and what's on it. No opinions.

Blog posts can be opinionated. That's where you make specific arguments about tools, approaches, or how things should be done. But even there, opinions should be intentional, not casual asides.

The rule: if you're going to state an opinion, do it on purpose and in the right place. Don't let opinions leak into descriptive copy where they don't belong.

## What to Embrace

- Specificity: name the tools, the numbers, the decisions
- Honest reflection on what did and didn't work
- The thinking behind decisions, not just the outcomes
- References to design principles when they apply naturally
- Short, direct sentences alongside longer analytical ones
- Questions that the reader might also be asking

## Audience

Like-minded builders and curious practitioners. People who build things for the sake of understanding how they work. People who care about doing things properly and who can appreciate the reasoning behind a project, not just the output. Fellow marketers, designers, and technologists who share a curiosity about how digital experiences shape behavior.

Write as if talking to a peer, not presenting to an audience.

## Structure Preferences

- Lead with the problem or observation, not with yourself
- Use headers that describe the content, not clickbait
- Keep paragraphs short (2 to 4 sentences)
- Let the natural structure of the topic guide the layout
- End with a takeaway or an open question, not a call-to-action pitch

## Example Rewrites

Original: "I'm a digital marketer who loves experimenting with new ideas and sharing what I learn along the way."
Revised: "I'm a digital marketer. I use AI coding tools to turn ideas into working products, and this site is where I document what I build and what I learn from building it."

Original: "There's something powerful about sharing your work openly."
Revised: Cut entirely. Don't make philosophical statements about the value of openness.

Original: "I document both because that's the honest thing to do."
Revised: "I document both." (Then revised further to cut entirely, because even "I document both" implies a promise.)

Original: "Every project here starts as an idea worth testing. Some turn into useful tools. Others become lessons."
Revised: Cut. This romanticizes the process. Just describe what the site contains.
